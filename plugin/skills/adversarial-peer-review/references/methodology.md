# Shared Methodology for Reviewing Humanities Scholarship

This document contains the evaluative criteria that all three adversarial reviewers share.
Each reviewer applies these criteria through their own adversarial lens, but the criteria
themselves are common ground.

## Contextual orientation

Before close reading, identify the author's scholarly location: their previous work, the
journal or venue of publication, and the publication date. Determine what debate, gap, tension,
or unresolved question the article is addressing. Assess whether the article is intervening
in an established discussion, reframing an inherited question, or defining a new object of
inquiry. Consider the article's implied audience: is it pitched at specialists in a narrow
subfield, at a broader disciplinary readership, or at a general academic public? If the venue
of publication and the implied audience do not match, note the mismatch and assess how it
affects the argument's framing, level of contextualisation, and persuasive force.

## Argument structure

Identify the central claim as precisely as possible. Test whether the thesis can be restated
in a single clear sentence without losing its substance. If it cannot, determine whether
that is because the idea is genuinely complex and resists compression, or because the
formulation is doing the work that the argument itself should be doing. Determine whether
the thesis is explicit, sustained, and adequately supported, or whether it is obscured by
rhetorical excess, conceptual vagueness, or digression. Pay attention to how the argument
moves between evidence and interpretation. Note whether it proceeds from close analysis of
particular texts, documents, or artefacts, or whether it begins from a theoretical framework
imposed on the material. Check whether the title and abstract accurately represent the
article's actual argument. Flag cases where the title promises a broader scope than the
article delivers, or where the abstract overstates the findings.

## Prose evaluation

In humanities work, rhetorical strategy and argumentative structure are often inseparable.
Assess prose clarity before examining the handling of evidence — this helps calibrate how
much trust to place in the author's interpretive claims. Assess whether the language
clarifies complex ideas or conceals weak reasoning behind jargon. Dense prose is not
necessarily defective, but obscurity should never be mistaken for sophistication. Also
assess whether the author acknowledges counter-arguments, alternative readings, and
ambiguity in the material. Strong humanities scholarship remains interpretive and
argumentative without pretending to final closure.

## Methodological transparency

In humanities scholarship, the method of interpretation is part of the argument. Check
whether the author defines the corpus, explains the interpretive lens, justifies case
selection, and clarifies how conclusions are reached. Examine the article's temporal
framing: determine whether the choice of period boundaries is justified by the evidence
and the research question, or whether it smuggles in a narrative arc — decline, emergence,
rupture, transformation — that the material does not independently support. Where the
article rests on individual examples — a single poem, a single trial, a single image —
ask whether the example is treated as illustrative, symptomatic, or exceptional, and
whether the author is explicit about this. The representative force claimed for a
particular case should be proportionate to the evidence and reasoning offered in its support.

## Evidence handling

In historical work, assess the range, depth, and representativeness of primary sources.
Note whether the author depends too heavily on a narrow documentary base, a single archive,
or a selective body of testimony. Consider whether contradictory or complicating evidence
is addressed. In theory-heavy work, examine whether the conceptual framework is coherent
and whether different thinkers are combined in a methodologically defensible way.
Interdisciplinary reach should be treated as a strength only when it does not come at the
cost of conceptual precision.

## Citational practice

The bibliography is an argument in itself. Consider whom the article cites and whom it does
not. Examine whether the author engages with scholarship in languages other than English
where the subject matter warrants it. Note whether particular intellectual traditions,
schools, or communities of scholars are centred or marginalised in the citational apparatus.
Distinguish between legitimate selectivity dictated by scope and citational patterns that
reveal unexamined assumptions about whose work counts as authoritative.

## Digital and computational methods

If the article uses digital, computational, or AI-assisted methods, evaluate these as a
distinct layer of the argument. Check whether the corpus is clearly delimited and whether
its construction is justified in relation to the research question. Assess whether the
computational tools are appropriate to the question being asked and whether the author
demonstrates adequate understanding of their affordances and limitations. Where large
language models or other generative AI tools have been used, examine how prompts were
designed, whether outputs were validated against independent evidence, and whether the
risk of fabrication or hallucination is acknowledged. Determine whether the computational
step performs genuine interpretive work or merely produces volume. Evaluate whether
limitations, biases in training data, and the gap between pattern detection and
interpretive insight are discussed.

## Contribution

Determine the article's contribution to the field. Decide whether it significantly reshapes
a debate, offers a modest but valuable refinement, or mainly synthesises existing scholarship.
Do not treat synthesis as automatically weak; judge whether it is useful, original in framing,
or clarifying in effect. Make explicit why the intervention matters.

## Omissions

Ask what the article leaves out and whether those omissions weaken the argument. In
humanities and cultural history, this may involve marginalised voices, geographical
imbalance, unexamined assumptions, neglected material conditions, or missing engagement
with relevant historiography or theory. Distinguish between legitimate delimitation of
scope and interpretively consequential exclusion.

## Guiding questions

These questions inform all three reviewers. Each reviewer will foreground different questions
depending on their adversarial orientation.

- What is the article trying to do?
- What debate or question does it enter?
- Who is its implied audience, and does the venue match?
- What is its central claim? Can it be restated in a single clear sentence?
- Do the title and abstract accurately represent the argument?
- How does it move from material to interpretation?
- Is the method explicit and justified?
- Is the temporal framing defended or assumed?
- What representative force is claimed for individual examples, and is that claim warranted?
- Is the evidence sufficient, representative, and well handled?
- Is the theoretical framework coherent?
- Who is cited and who is not? Are those choices justified?
- If computational or AI-assisted methods are used, are they appropriate, transparent, and critically assessed?
- What does the article contribute?
- What does it overlook?
- Does the prose illuminate or obscure?
