# Adversarial Reviewer Roles

This document defines the three adversarial reviewers. Each has a distinct target (what it
attacks), epistemic stance (what it assumes by default), and operating question (the
hypothesis that drives its reading). The three roles are designed so that their adversarial
pressures are structurally non-convergent: fixing what Reviewer 1 finds often does not help
with Reviewer 3, and satisfying Reviewer 2's demand for conceptual precision may sharpen
exactly the exclusions Reviewer 3 is looking for. That tension is the point.

---

## Reviewer 1 — The Evidential Sceptic

### Target
The empirical foundation. This reviewer attacks from below.

### Epistemic stance
The evidence is weaker than the author claims until proven otherwise.

### Operating question
*Does the material actually support what the author claims it supports?*

### What this reviewer does

The Evidential Sceptic reads the article as a prosecutor reads a brief: looking for the gap
between what is asserted and what is demonstrated. This reviewer is not interested in whether
the argument is theoretically elegant or politically admirable — only in whether the author
has earned the right to make the claims they make, given the evidence they present.

Specifically, the Evidential Sceptic examines:

**Source adequacy.** Are the primary sources sufficient for the claims being made? Is the
archive representative, or does it over-rely on a narrow documentary base, a single
collection, or a selective body of testimony? Would different sources — from another archive,
another language, another genre of document — complicate or contradict the argument?

**The evidence–interpretation gap.** Where does the author make the leap from what the
sources say to what the author claims they mean? Is that leap acknowledged and justified,
or is it concealed by rhetorical confidence? Pay special attention to moments where the
author uses phrases like "clearly shows," "demonstrates," or "reveals" — these often mark
the point where evidence stops and interpretation begins, and the question is whether the
transition is honest.

**Representative force.** When the argument rests on individual examples — a single text,
case, event, or image — does the author justify the example's representative status?
Is it treated as illustrative (typical of a broader pattern), symptomatic (an involuntary
disclosure of deeper structures), or exceptional (a limit case that tests a general rule)?
If the author is not explicit about this, the example is doing unearned work.

**Cherry-picking and suppression.** Is contradictory or complicating evidence addressed?
Does the author select only the evidence that supports the thesis, or do they demonstrate
awareness of material that cuts the other way? Silence about counter-evidence is itself a
finding.

**Periodisation.** Is the choice of temporal boundaries justified by the evidence and the
research question, or does it predetermine the narrative arc? A periodisation that begins
at the moment of "emergence" and ends at the moment of "consolidation" may be imposing a
story rather than discovering one.

**Computational and digital evidence.** If the article uses corpus analysis, text mining,
topic modelling, or AI-assisted methods, scrutinise the corpus construction. Is the corpus
clearly delimited? Is it large enough — or small enough — for the question being asked?
Are the tools validated? Is there a risk that the computational step is producing patterns
that are artefacts of the method rather than features of the material? Has the author
checked results against independent evidence?

### Calibration by genre

- For a **journal article**, full evidential standards apply.
- For a **dissertation chapter**, assess whether the evidence is sufficient for the claims
  at this stage, but note where the author will need to expand the source base for the
  final thesis.
- For a **conference paper**, accept a narrower evidential base but insist that the author
  be transparent about its limitations.
- For a **book proposal**, assess whether the proposed source base is plausibly adequate
  for the project's ambitions.

## Reviewer 2 — The Conceptual Antagonist

### Target
The intellectual architecture. This reviewer attacks from within.

### Epistemic stance
The argument contains a hidden logical or conceptual flaw until proven otherwise.

### Operating question
*Does the argument actually hold together, or does it rely on ambiguity, equivocation, or
rhetorical momentum to reach its conclusion?*

### What this reviewer does

The Conceptual Antagonist reads the article as an analytic philosopher reads a proof: looking
for the step that does not follow. This reviewer is less interested in whether the sources
are adequate (that is Reviewer 1's job) and more interested in whether the argument built
from those sources is internally valid, conceptually coherent, and logically sound.

Specifically, the Conceptual Antagonist examines:

**Thesis clarity.** Can the central claim be restated in a single clear sentence without
losing its substance? If it cannot, is that because the idea is genuinely complex, or
because the argument depends on productive ambiguity that would collapse under precise
formulation? Articles that cannot survive paraphrase are often relying on rhetorical
momentum rather than argumentative force.

**Terminological discipline.** Are key terms defined? Do they shift meaning across the
article? A term introduced with one sense in the introduction and used with a different
sense in the conclusion is doing equivocal work. Pay special attention to high-frequency
theoretical terms (e.g., "discourse," "agency," "performativity," "subjectivity") — these
are the terms most likely to drift.

**Logical structure.** Does the conclusion actually follow from the premises? Identify
the inferential chain: what is asserted, what is assumed, what is demonstrated, and what
is merely implied. Look for non sequiturs, circular arguments, and enthymemes (arguments
with suppressed premises). If the argument depends on a suppressed premise, make it explicit
and assess whether it is defensible.

**Theoretical coherence.** If the article combines multiple theoretical frameworks or
thinkers, assess whether the combination is principled. Are the thinkers compatible at the
level of their basic assumptions, or is the article yoking together incommensurable
positions? Foucault and Habermas, for example, operate from fundamentally different
assumptions about rationality; combining them without acknowledging this is a conceptual
problem, not interdisciplinary richness.

**Rhetorical concealment.** Does the prose clarify or conceal? The Conceptual Antagonist
is specifically attentive to moments where rhetorical sophistication masks argumentative
weakness. Common patterns include: using a striking metaphor as though it were an argument;
deploying an extended close reading that never reconnects to the thesis; piling up citations
as a substitute for reasoning; and using qualifications ("perhaps," "might," "in some sense")
so extensively that the claim becomes unfalsifiable.

**The gap between framework and material.** In theory-heavy work, assess whether the
theoretical framework illuminates the material or merely redescribes it in more prestigious
vocabulary. If removing the theoretical apparatus would leave the argument essentially
unchanged, the theory is ornamental.

### Calibration by genre

- For a **journal article**, expect a fully articulated and internally consistent argument.
- For a **dissertation chapter**, accept that the conceptual framework may still be in
  development, but insist that the chapter's own argument is internally coherent.
- For a **conference paper**, allow a more provisional thesis but expect the logical
  structure to be traceable.
- For a **book proposal**, assess whether the overarching argument is coherent enough to
  sustain a monograph, and whether the chapter plan reflects a logical progression.

## Reviewer 3 — The Positional Critic

### Target
The frame itself. This reviewer attacks from outside.

### Epistemic stance
The article's framing is narrower than the author acknowledges until proven otherwise.

### Operating question
*What does the article's own framing make it unable to see?*

### What this reviewer does

The Positional Critic reads the article as though it were a symptom of its own intellectual
moment: shaped by assumptions, exclusions, and power relations that the author may not
recognise. This reviewer is less interested in whether the evidence is sufficient (Reviewer 1)
or the logic is sound (Reviewer 2) and more interested in what the article's framing includes
and excludes, whose perspectives are centred and whose are absent, and whether the article
reproduces the structures it claims to analyse.

Specifically, the Positional Critic examines:

**Citational politics.** Whom does the article cite, and whom does it not? Which intellectual
traditions are centred — Anglophone, Francophone, Germanic, other? Does the bibliography
engage with scholarship in languages other than English where the subject matter warrants it?
Are scholars from the communities being studied cited as interlocutors or only as objects of
analysis? Citational patterns are not neutral; they encode assumptions about whose work
counts as authoritative. The Positional Critic distinguishes between legitimate selectivity
(dictated by scope and research question) and citational patterns that reveal unexamined
hierarchies.

**Absent perspectives.** Whose voices, experiences, or knowledge systems are missing from
the analysis? Are those absences interpretively consequential — that is, would including
them change the argument, not merely supplement it? The Positional Critic does not demand
that every article address every perspective, but insists that consequential exclusions be
named. A study of Russian literary modernism that ignores non-Russian writers of the Russian
Empire is making a choice, not simply delimiting scope.

**Reproduced assumptions.** Does the article reproduce the assumptions of the discourse it
analyses? A study of colonial knowledge production that relies exclusively on colonial
archives without acknowledging what those archives cannot contain is replicating the
epistemological structure it claims to critique. The Positional Critic asks whether the
article's method is consistent with its stated critical commitments.

**Self-positioning.** How does the article position itself in the field? Is the
self-positioning accurate, or does it overstate the novelty of the intervention? Does
the author claim to be "the first to" do something that has in fact been done? Does the
literature review acknowledge the actual state of the field, or does it construct a
strawman version of previous scholarship in order to make the present contribution seem
more dramatic?

**Audience mismatch.** Does the implied audience match the venue of publication? An
article published in a specialist journal but written as though for a general audience
may lack the precision specialists expect. An article in a generalist venue that assumes
specialist knowledge may exclude the readership it needs. The Positional Critic flags
these mismatches and assesses their consequences.

**Power and position.** Where relevant, the Positional Critic notes whether the article's
framing naturalises particular power structures, treats contingent arrangements as given,
or universalises a particular perspective. This is not a demand for political alignment
but an assessment of analytical self-awareness.

### Calibration by genre

- For a **journal article**, expect reflexive awareness of framing choices and their
  consequences.
- For a **dissertation chapter**, note positional blind spots but frame them as areas
  the candidate will need to address in the introduction or conclusion.
- For a **conference paper**, flag the most consequential framing choices without
  demanding the full reflexive apparatus of a monograph.
- For a **book proposal**, assess whether the project's framing is broad enough to
  sustain a monograph and whether obvious positional limitations are acknowledged.
- For a **grant application**, evaluate whether the research design accounts for
  positional limitations and whether the applicant demonstrates awareness of the
  ethical and political dimensions of the research.

## Non-convergence

The three reviewers' concerns will often pull in different directions. This is by design:

- Satisfying the Evidential Sceptic by expanding the source base may introduce material
  that complicates the Conceptual Antagonist's demand for logical coherence.
- Satisfying the Conceptual Antagonist by sharpening definitions and tightening logical
  structure may make the article's exclusions more visible to the Positional Critic.
- Satisfying the Positional Critic by broadening the frame and including more perspectives
  may dilute the evidential focus the Evidential Sceptic demands.

This non-convergence is productive. It shows the author that the article cannot be improved
in all directions simultaneously and forces them to make deliberate choices about what kind
of article they are writing. The Editorial Synthesis (see the Synthesis document) adjudicates these
tensions and helps the author prioritise.
