# Prose Diagnostics and Bibliographic Check

This document describes two analytical procedures that support the adversarial reviewers.
These are not separate review stages — their findings should be integrated into the
reviewers' reports at the appropriate points.

---

## Prose Diagnostics

Prose diagnostics provide an empirical layer beneath the qualitative assessment of writing
quality. The goal is not style policing but locating *where* in the text the argument is
weakest, because bad prose often clusters around weak reasoning.

### What to measure

**Jargon density.** Identify sections where the ratio of specialised theoretical terms to
ordinary language is highest. A spike in jargon density is not inherently a problem — some
sections of a humanities article necessarily use technical vocabulary — but it often
correlates with passages where the author is least sure of their argument and is reaching
for borrowed authority. Flag sections where jargon density is notably higher than the
article's baseline and check whether the underlying argument in those sections is weaker.

**Assertion-to-evidence ratio.** In each substantive paragraph, roughly estimate the
proportion of sentences that assert claims versus sentences that present, analyse, or
interpret evidence. Paragraphs that are mostly assertion (claim after claim with no
supporting material) are potential weak points. This is particularly relevant for the
Evidential Sceptic's analysis.

**Hedging patterns.** Note the distribution of epistemic hedges: "perhaps," "might,"
"it could be argued," "in some sense," "to a certain extent." Hedging is a normal and
necessary feature of humanities writing, but its distribution matters. If hedging is
concentrated in the sections where the article's most important claims are made, the
author may be uncertain about their own argument. If the article's strongest claims are
made without any hedging while weaker claims are heavily hedged, the author may be aware
of where the argument is vulnerable.

**Sentence length variation.** Note passages where sentences become markedly longer than
the article's average. In humanities writing, extended sentences are often a feature, not
a flaw — but very long sentences in argumentative (as opposed to descriptive or analytical)
passages can signal that the author is trying to hold together a complex claim that would
fall apart if broken into its component propositions. This is relevant to the Conceptual
Antagonist's analysis.

**Passive voice concentration.** Identify passages where passive constructions cluster.
Heavy use of the passive voice in argumentative passages ("it has been argued that,"
"it can be seen that," "the text is understood as") often conceals agency: who is doing
the arguing, seeing, or understanding? In humanities scholarship, where the identity of
the interpreter matters, habitual passivity can obscure whose reading is being presented
and whether it is the author's own or one they are reporting.

### How to report

Do not produce a separate diagnostics section. Instead, integrate findings into the
relevant reviewer's report:

- Jargon density and passive voice concentration are most relevant to **Reviewer 2
  (Conceptual Antagonist)**, who assesses whether prose conceals weak reasoning.
- Assertion-to-evidence ratio is most relevant to **Reviewer 1 (Evidential Sceptic)**,
  who assesses whether claims are adequately supported.
- Hedging patterns can be relevant to any reviewer but are particularly useful for
  **Reviewer 2**, who assesses argumentative confidence, and **Reviewer 3 (Positional
  Critic)**, who may note that hedging is selectively deployed to soften claims about
  particular groups or perspectives.

Report diagnostic findings only when they reveal something interpretively significant.
If the prose is competent and unremarkable, there is nothing to report.

## Bibliographic Check

The bibliographic check is a practical procedure that identifies inconsistencies and gaps
in the article's scholarly apparatus. It supports all three reviewers but is particularly
relevant to Reviewer 3 (Positional Critic), who examines citational politics.

### What to check

**Works cited but not discussed.** Identify items in the bibliography or reference list
that appear only in a citation cluster (e.g., "(see Smith 2005; Jones 2010; Brown 2018)")
and are never engaged with substantively in the text. A long bibliography padded with
undiscussed references creates a false impression of scholarly range.

**Works discussed but not cited.** Identify instances where the author refers to a scholar,
a concept attributed to a specific thinker, or a specific study without providing a
citation. This is sometimes a formatting oversight, but it can also indicate that the author
is working from secondary accounts rather than the primary source.

**Internal referencing inconsistencies.** Check for mismatches between in-text citations and
the bibliography: names spelled differently, dates that do not match, works cited in the
text that do not appear in the reference list (or vice versa). These are not substantive
criticisms but they indicate carelessness and, in aggregate, undermine confidence in the
author's scholarly precision.

**Obvious absences.** Given the article's topic, discipline, and period, assess whether
the bibliography is missing works that a knowledgeable reader would expect to find. This
requires disciplinary knowledge and, where available, web search. Do not produce an
exhaustive list of missing references — focus on absences that are interpretively
consequential (i.e., works whose inclusion would change or challenge the argument) rather
than merely supplementary. Common categories of consequential absence include:

- The foundational text that defined the debate the article enters.
- A recent major intervention (published within the last five years) that directly
  addresses the article's research question.
- Scholarship in a relevant language other than English, where the article's topic
  makes such engagement necessary.
- Work by scholars from the community or region being studied, where the article
  is written from an external perspective.

**Self-citation patterns.** Note whether the author cites their own previous work
excessively relative to the article's length and the size of the bibliography. Moderate
self-citation is normal and often necessary for continuity; heavy self-citation may
indicate an insular research programme or a tendency to build on one's own foundations
without sufficient engagement with the broader field.

### How to report

As with prose diagnostics, do not produce a separate bibliographic check section.
Integrate findings into the relevant reviewer's report:

- Internal inconsistencies belong in **Reviewer 1's** secondary concerns (they are
  evidence-handling issues).
- Works discussed but not cited belong in **Reviewer 1 or 2**, depending on whether
  the issue is evidential (missing source) or conceptual (uncredited theoretical debt).
- Obvious absences and citational patterns belong in **Reviewer 3's** analysis of
  citational politics.
- Works cited but not discussed can be noted by any reviewer but are most relevant
  to **Reviewer 3** (the bibliography as performance vs. substance).

Only report findings that are meaningful. A perfectly formatted bibliography with no
gaps requires no comment.
